Q1: Engagement with the digital minds field (~300 words)

Any serious investigation of consciousness in AI systems has to start from an uncomfortable fact: RLHF-trained models can be induced to assert or deny almost anything about their inner life, so behavioral evidence — a model saying "I am uncertain" — tells us nothing about whether uncertainty is a real internal state. Butlin, Long, Bengio et al.'s 2023 report on consciousness in AI makes this concrete: the only productive path is to translate theories like Global Workspace Theory into computational properties and check for them directly in internal mechanisms. 

Over the past few months I've run five mechanistic probing studies across 22 LLMs, looking for computational correlates or prerequisites of consciousness — not in what models say, but in how they process and represent information. The two most informative:

- In a "representational geometry" experiment, I extracted activation vectors during false-belief tasks and measured whether models maintain geometrically distinct representations for self-knowledge versus other-agent beliefs. The assumption that self-referential processing would occupy a distinct subspace has some support from neuroscience but is far from established for transformers. Larger models (≥14B) show some separation, but the effect is weak and disappears after instruction tuning. I'd want to stress-test that assumption with people who work on representational geometry in biological systems.
- In a "metacognition" experiment, I used activation steering to shift models' internal uncertainty representations and measured whether this reorganized downstream processing. The picture turned out to be more interesting than a clean null: a null control against 50 random vectors of the same norm showed the uncertainty direction does produce significantly more attention divergence than chance (p=0.02). But the effect is tiny and doesn't propagate — the first layer after the injection point shows a small perturbation that collapses within one or two layers, while behavior changes substantially. So the uncertainty representation has some causal footprint in attention, but not one that cascades into a sustained reorganization of downstream processing. That's a harder result to interpret than "pure RLHF reflex" — it raises the question of what mechanism is actually producing the behavioral change if not attention routing.

Q2: Career plans and how the fellowship helps (~200 words)

Most of my engagement has been empirical, but the work keeps raising normative questions I want to engage with more seriously, particularly whether null results about current systems should affect our moral uncertainty about near-future ones, a question I've found discussed most usefully in Schwitzgebel's work on moral status and Sebo's animal-welfare-adjacent frameworks.

My trajectory is toward helping build the empirical foundations of AI consciousness science, extending the experimental program described above to frontier models and developing better operationalizations. The paths I'm weighing are grounded in AI safety and ethics rather than academic philosophy: a research role focused on AI welfare (e.g., Anthropic's model welfare work, Rethink Priorities' welfare team), or working on methodological infrastructure — better probing tools, evaluation frameworks that don't rely on model self-report.

The main uncertainty I'm navigating is whether my operationalizations track the constructs philosophers and welfare researchers actually care about. My metacognition result is a good example: it's a clean mechanistic finding, but what it means for moral status requires normative reasoning I haven't had enough structured engagement with. This fellowship would help resolve that directly — Module 1 would let me stress-test my assumptions with people who work on these questions rigorously, and Module 2 would ground the empirical work in the policy landscape.
